Section 2: Treating possession of nukes as immoral increases pressure for nuclear constraint that reduce deterrence effects in regional hotspots.

Subpoint 1: Pushes governments/NGOs/institutions towards nuke reduction norms

Evidence Card 1: Moral Framing and Nuclear Reduction Norms

Treating nuclear weapons as immoral fundamentally shifts the debate from strategic or legal arguments to ethical necessity. This moral framing highlights the catastrophic humanitarian effects of nuclear weapons, thereby delegitimizing them and increasing the stigma associated with their possession. This stigma can make it harder for governments to openly endorse nuclear arsenals, increase political costs, and weaken the prestige of nuclear possession. It also empowers NGOs to mobilize public opinion and build grassroots campaigns, connecting disarmament to human rights and humanitarian law. International institutions and expert networks further reinforce this by condemning possession and use, promoting treaties like the TPNW, and embedding anti-nuclear ethics in discourse.

  • 🔗 Reference Link: gsinstitute.org

Evidence Card 2: The Catholic Church's Role in Nuclear Disarmament

The Catholic Church, among other religious bodies, plays a significant role in advancing nuclear disarmament by affirming that the use, threat of use, and even possession of nuclear weapons are immoral. This stance challenges the traditional doctrine of deterrence and puts moral pressure on nuclear-reliant states. By framing nuclear weapons as morally unacceptable, the Church contributes to strengthening global norms against their existence and use, pushing towards reduction and eventual elimination.

  • 🔗 Reference Link: nti.org

Subpoint 2: Leads to arms control pressure, sanctions, deterrence weaking policies, isolationist frameworks

Evidence Card 1: Moral Delegitimization and Arms Control

Perceiving nuclear weapons as immoral or illegitimate strengthens the argument for arms control. Discourse that frames nuclear weapons as "wrongheaded and immoral" or "dangerous items that must be dismantled" reinforces the normative unacceptability of nuclear use. This moral stance can bolster the legitimacy of arms control efforts by providing an ethical foundation for limiting and reducing nuclear arsenals. However, the effectiveness of sanctions, particularly punitive ones, can be undermined if they lead to isolation rather than engagement, potentially weakening long-term nonproliferation outcomes.

  • 🔗 Reference Link: armscontrol.org

Evidence Card 2: Nuclear Disarmament and Moral Overtones

The discourse surrounding nuclear disarmament often carries a strong moral overtone, portraying nuclear weapons as inherently dangerous and immoral. This moral perspective suggests that, regardless of their military value, these weapons should be dismantled. While such moral arguments can support arms control and delegitimize nuclear possession, the effectiveness of related policies like sanctions depends on whether they address the underlying security concerns of proliferating states. Overreliance on purely punitive measures without considering security drivers may prove counterproductive to nonproliferation goals.

  • 🔗 Reference Link: oxfordre.com

Subpoint 3: Deterrence depends on perceived acceptability of nuke use threat

Evidence Card 1: Conditional Acceptability of Nuclear Threats

The perceived acceptability of nuclear use threats is conditional, contested, and scenario-dependent. While publics may tolerate these threats as a component of deterrence, acceptance significantly diminishes when the threat is viewed as non-credible, disproportionate, targeted at non-nuclear scenarios, or inconsistent with humanitarian norms. Research, such as a study on Japanese public opinion, indicates ambivalence, with support for extended deterrence existing alongside a long-term aspiration for abolition. This highlights a nuanced public perception where the threat of use is accepted for deterrence but not for actual employment, especially in humanitarian terms.

  • 🔗 Reference Link: chathamhouse.org

Evidence Card 2: Credibility and Morality in Deterrence

Nuclear deterrence relies on the credible threat of actual use, but this credibility is often debated on moral and practical grounds. Proponents argue that a believable threat is essential for deterrence, while critics contend that such threats are inherently dangerous and often lack true credibility, particularly if directed at non-nuclear aggression. The moral and legal problems associated with the threat to use nuclear weapons can undermine their perceived acceptability, especially when humanitarian consequences are considered. This tension between strategic necessity and moral acceptability shapes how effective a nuclear threat is perceived to be.

  • 🔗 Reference Link: gotech.spp.umd.edu

Subpoint 4: Even 'soft' norm shifts change military plans

Evidence Card 1: Norm Erosion and Military Doctrine Shifts

Soft norm shifts can significantly influence military nuclear plans by gradually making certain uses, postures, or capabilities of nuclear weapons appear more acceptable or less stigmatized. When norms against nuclear use weaken, military planners may begin to view nuclear employment as a more realistic option rather than an absolute last resort. This can lead to changes in military doctrine, shifting towards concepts like "early use," "limited use," or "tailored use" strategies. Such shifts blur the distinction between nuclear and conventional conflict, encouraging the integration of nuclear forces and potentially leading to the development of lower-yield warheads or more flexible delivery platforms to fit these new doctrines.

  • 🔗 Reference Link: amacad.org

Evidence Card 2: Russia's Evolving Nuclear Doctrine and Norm Shifts

Russia's evolving nuclear doctrine exemplifies how soft norm shifts can alter military plans. Russia has been observed to be giving significant thought to the employment of its nuclear weapons in a conflict scenario with NATO, with interpretations suggesting a move towards limited nuclear use to deter or halt conventional attacks. This demonstrates a strategic shift influenced by an erosion of historical norms against nuclear use. As the stigma surrounding nuclear use declines and technology evolves, military leaders may perceive a need for more visible readiness, survivable forces, or 'usable' weapons, further destabilizing strategic planning.

  • 🔗 Reference Link: frstrategie.org
